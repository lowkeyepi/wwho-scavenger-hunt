Tuberculosis (TB) is an ancient infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis that primarily affects the lungs but can affect other parts of the body. While TB has existed for thousands of years, drug-resistant forms have emerged as a major global health threat. Multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) is resistant to at least two of the most powerful first-line anti-TB drugs (isoniazid and rifampicin). Extensively drug-resistant TB (XDR-TB) is resistant to even more drugs, making it extremely difficult to treat. Drug resistance develops when TB treatment is not completed properly, when the wrong medications are used, or when the drug supply is irregular. Treatment for drug-resistant TB can take 18-24 months or longer and involves more toxic medications with serious side effects. The disease spreads through the air when infected individuals cough, sneeze, or speak. Factors contributing to drug-resistant TB include inadequate treatment programs, poor adherence to treatment regimens, and lack of access to proper diagnostics.
