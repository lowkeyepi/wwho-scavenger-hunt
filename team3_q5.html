Dengue fever is a mosquito-borne viral disease transmitted primarily by Aedes aegypti mosquitoes. The disease is endemic in over 100 tropical and subtropical countries, with approximately half of the world's population at risk. Dengue causes flu-like symptoms including high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, joint and muscle pain, and rash. Most people recover within a week, but some develop severe dengue (previously called dengue hemorrhagic fever), which can be life-threatening and involves plasma leakage, severe bleeding, and organ impairment. Climate change, urbanization, and international travel have contributed to dengue's expansion into new areas, including parts of southern Europe and the southern United States. There are four distinct serotypes of the dengue virus (DENV-1, DENV-2, DENV-3, DENV-4), and infection with one serotype provides lifelong immunity to that serotype but only temporary immunity to the others. Subsequent infections with different serotypes increase the risk of severe disease.
