Ebola virus disease (EVD) is a severe, often fatal illness caused by the Ebola virus, first discovered in 1976 near the Ebola River in what is now the Democratic Republic of Congo. The disease causes fever, severe headache, muscle pain, weakness, and in severe cases, bleeding both internally and externally. Ebola spreads through direct contact with blood or body fluids of infected individuals or animals, particularly fruit bats, which are believed to be natural reservoir hosts. The 2014-2016 West Africa outbreak was the largest in history, affecting Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone, with over 28,000 cases. Healthcare workers are at particularly high risk due to their close contact with infected patients. Modern treatment includes supportive care and newer therapies like monoclonal antibodies, and vaccines have been developed that show promising effectiveness.
